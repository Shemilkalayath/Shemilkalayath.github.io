Istanbul, Turkey · January 2025

Between Two Continents

A week in Istanbul — the bazaars, the bridges, and the Bosphorus in winter.

Istanbul in January is cold, grey, and almost completely stripped of tourists. Which is exactly when you should go. The Grand Bazaar, usually a crush of selfie sticks and tour groups, becomes something closer to what it must have felt like a century ago — a working market, full of traders, tea glasses, and the low hum of commerce.

I spent my first full day doing what I always do in a new city: walking until my feet hurt and eating whatever looked good. In Istanbul this means simit from a cart on the bridge, a bowl of lentil soup somewhere in Eminönü, and strong black tea in glass cups, refilled without asking.

There is something about the Galata Bridge that stays with you. Fishermen line both sides at all hours, their lines dropping into the Golden Horn below. Underneath, restaurants blast pop music nobody is listening to. The whole thing is slightly absurd and completely alive.

I crossed to the Asian side on my third day, on the ferry from Eminönü to Kadıköy. The crossing takes twenty minutes. The city looks completely different from the water — the minarets, the hills, the jumbled Ottoman and modern rooflines tumbling down to the shore. I understand why people have been writing about Istanbul for centuries. It earns the attention.

Kadıköy is where Istanbul feels most like a city rather than a monument. The market there — the Kadıköy Bazaar — is a covered maze of fishmongers, spice sellers, delicatessens, and tiny restaurants. I ate a fish sandwich at a counter with four stools and no menu and it was perfect.
